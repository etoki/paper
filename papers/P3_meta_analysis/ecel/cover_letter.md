## Manuscript summary

This paper reports a modality-stratified random-effects meta-regression (REML + Hartung–Knapp adjustment) of 13 primary studies (combined N ≈ 6,200) drawn from a corpus of 31 Big Five × online-learning studies. The headline result is a **modality × Extraversion interaction** that is not detectable in any pooled estimate to date in this literature: the Extraversion–achievement correlation is *negative* in fully-asynchronous online settings (r ≈ −0.13, k = 6) and *near zero or weakly positive* in synchronous and blended settings (r ≈ +0.02 to +0.05, k = 5). A modality × trait interaction model with HKSJ adjustment confirms that the contrast is statistically robust (Q_between p < .001 for E; n.s. for the four other traits, confirming that the effect is Extraversion-specific). The paper interprets this asymmetry as a self-regulated-learning-load effect: asynchronous formats penalise learners whose preferred mode of engagement is interpersonal interaction.

## Fit with the ECEL scope

ECEL's scope explicitly foregrounds modality (synchronous / asynchronous / blended) as a first-order moderator of e-learning outcomes. The paper's contribution is a quantitative synthesis on exactly that axis, with a level of methodological detail (PRISMA 2020 flow, REML pooling primitives, three sensitivity scenarios, and a transparent COI flag for the author's own primary study A-25 Tokiwa 2025) that aims to set a reproducibility benchmark for trait × modality syntheses in e-learning research.
